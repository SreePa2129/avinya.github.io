Exploratory data analysis (EDA) is an important step in analyzing greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions data. EDA involves examining the data to identify patterns, trends, and relationships, as well as checking for data quality issues such as missing values, outliers, and errors. Overall, EDA is an important step in understanding GHG emissions data and identifying patterns and trends.

Data Gathering

The data is collected from Climate watch portal. Used the following API to gather the required data from Climate watch portal https://www.climatewatchdata.org/
API used: https://www.climatewatchdata.org/api/v1/data/historical_emissions?gases=[%27ch4%27,%27co2%27]

Graphs showing outliers.


Graphs that show outliers in GHG emissions raw dataset can help identify years vs emissions that have significantly higher or lower emissions compared to other countries in the year. Outliers are data points that are significantly different from other data points in the dataset. Outliers in GHG emissions data can be caused by factors such as the size of a country's economy, population, and the types of industries it has.
In this example, we can see the distribution of GHG emissions for all years in the dataset. The box represents the interquartile range (the middle 50% of the data), and the whiskers extend to the highest and lowest values within 1.5 times the interquartile range. The dots represent outliers, which are data points that are more than 1.5 times the interquartile range away from the nearest quartile. We can see that Qatar has significantly higher emissions than all other countries in the dataset.

Graphs showing distribution of emissions across the countries with uncleaned dataset.

In this example, we can see the distribution of GHG emissions by country and sector in 2017, according to data from the World Resources Institute. Each rectangle represents a country, and the size of the rectangle corresponds to the total emissions for that country. The color of the rectangle represents the sector responsible for the majority of emissions (e.g. blue for electricity and heat production, yellow for agriculture). We can see that China and the United States are the largest emitters overall, and that electricity and heat production is the largest source of emissions globally. However, there are also some smaller countries that have relatively high emissions per capita.

Graphs showing distribution of emissions across the countries with cleaned dataset.

Here are some types of graphs that can show the distribution of greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions for CO2, F gas, methane, and nitrogen oxides (NOx) across countries with a cleaned dataset.
In this example, we can see the distribution of CO2, F gas, methane, and NOx emissions for the top 10 emitters in 2019, according to data from the European Environment Agency. China emitted the most CO2 (27.6% of global emissions), followed by the United States (14.5%), India (7.5%), and Russia (4.9%). However, the United States emitted the most methane (16.2% of global emissions), followed by China (15.4%), India (6.9%), and Russia (6.8%). The other countries in the top 10 emitted varying amounts of different types of GHGs.
